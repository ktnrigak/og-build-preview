The business in plain English

OG Build is software for construction. General contractors use it to manage their projects and coordinate with the subcontractors they hire. Subcontractors use it to see what work they have across all their contractors in one place. The software is free for basic use. Contractors pay when they want features like customer estimates, templates, and a dashboard showing which projects need attention.

The business grows in a self-reinforcing way. Every contractor who signs up invites their subs. Those subs also work for OTHER contractors who are not on the platform yet, and they invite those contractors too. Each new contractor then invites their own subs, and the cycle continues. We do not pay for marketing. The product spreads because people use it as part of their normal workday.

This page lets you test different assumptions about how fast that spread happens and what it produces in users and revenue over 5 years.

The Big Picture

What this means for the business

The charts above are just numbers. Here is what those numbers mean in business terms, both short and long term.

Short term: months 1-12

Year 1 is about proving the software works with our founding contractor, Oleg, then carefully bringing in more contractors through direct outreach. The free tier is intentionally generous so contractors can run their whole business on it before they ever pay. Most of them will stay free for the first 3 months while they get dependent on the product.

Revenue is small in year 1. That is by design. The point of year 1 is to build a foundation of happy users who will tell other contractors about the software. Think of it like a restaurant's first 6 months: you are not trying to be profitable yet, you are trying to become the place people recommend.

The key metric to watch in year 1 is NOT revenue. It is whether contractors invite their subs and whether those subs invite their OTHER contractors. If that cycle works, year 2 takes care of itself.

Medium term: years 2 and 3

Starting in year 2, the word-of-mouth cycle meaningfully contributes to growth on top of direct sales. Contractors who became dependent on the free tier in year 1 start upgrading to paid plans. Revenue begins to compound.

By year 3, the software is becoming the default way construction work gets coordinated in its target market. Contractors who do not use it are the exception, not the rule. This is when new revenue streams become possible beyond subscriptions: a marketplace for contractors to find reliable subs, payment processing on estimates, insurance referrals, supplier partnerships. None of those are in the model above.

Long term: years 4 and 5

Years 4 and 5 are when the compounding becomes dramatic. The user base built in years 1-3 keeps feeding the word-of-mouth cycle, and by now the business has a track record of happy customers that make outbound sales significantly easier. Most of the paying customers signed up in years 2-3 are still on the platform, and revenue has had time to accumulate.

This is also when the second wave of revenue streams can start contributing meaningfully: the marketplace becomes valuable because there are enough contractors and subs on the platform for matching to work. Payment processing on estimates starts showing up as a revenue line. Partnerships with suppliers and insurers get easier to sign because the platform has real traction.

A typical software business at the 5-year mark trades at 4x to 8x annual revenue. Check the "Yearly revenue rate" chart to see what that would value the business at in each scenario. The auto-calculated valuation at the top of this section uses the current scenario's year 5 revenue.

What could go wrong

The biggest risk is not technical. It is whether subcontractors actually tell their other contractors about the software. If they do not, the word-of-mouth cycle breaks and we are back to being a normal software company paying for every customer through sales and ads. Drag the "Saved subs who reverse-invite" slider to 5% to see what that looks like.

The second risk is that contractors never upgrade from free. The free tier is generous on purpose, but if nobody ever pays, the whole thing is a charity. Drag the "Contractor Pro conversion" slider to 5% to see that scenario.

Both risks are testable in the first 6 months with our pilot contractor. If either one fails the test, we know early and can adjust before spending significant money.

How to compare this to real construction businesses

A mid-sized general contractor running 15-20 projects typically generates $3M-$8M in annual revenue but nets only 5-8% after costs. A software business like OG Build has totally different economics: once the software is built, each new customer costs almost nothing to serve.

That means a software business with $1M in annual revenue is worth more than a construction business with $5M in annual revenue, because the software revenue is recurring, scales without hiring, and holds its value if someone wants to buy it.

The charts above show a range from a small but healthy business to a meaningful company in 5 years. The path beyond year 5 — marketplace, payment processing, insurance, partnerships — is where this becomes a large business.
